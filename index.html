Founder & Product Lead
Project Bluebonnet
Turning phones into a reason to go outside instead of stay in

Bluebonnet turns your phone into a reason to go outside. Explore state parks, identify species in real time, find geocaches, and learn about the land around you.

The goal is simple: make parks feel approachable for people who have never felt like they were for them.

How It Started

It started on a hike when we ran out of things to talk about. None of us knew what we were looking at or how to engage with it, and we kept coming back to the same idea: what if nature was something you could actively interact with, not just walk through?

At first, that sounded like a simple idea. But the more I paid attention, the more it felt like something bigger. People weren't avoiding parks because they weren't interested. They just didn't know where to start or what they would get out of it.

I spent the next few months testing that assumption. I road tripped through Zion, Death Valley, Guadalupe Mountains, and Caprock Canyon, talking to rangers and first-time visitors. That experience reshaped the product entirely.

Why This Matters

Most people drive past state parks without stopping. Not because they don't want to, but because they don't know what they'll get out of it. The problem isn't access. It's uncertainty.

But there's a second problem that doesn't get talked about enough. Parks depend on visitors to stay funded and protected. When people stop showing up, the case for preserving these places gets harder to make.

Bluebonnet is built for both sides of that. Get more people outside. Keep them coming back. Make the experience feel worth it every time.

The phone isn't the problem. Passive use is.
People aren't going to spend less time on their phones. The question is what that time leads to. Bluebonnet redirects passive screen time into active outdoor exploration. A reason to go somewhere, not just scroll past it.
Discovery & Research

My research wasn't about features. It was about behavior. I wanted to understand what actually happens between the moment someone considers going to a park and the moment they either show up or don't.

Most people hit the same wall. They couldn't picture what they'd actually do once they got there. They didn't know what things cost. And once they arrived, they walked through without knowing what they were looking at.

What I Observed
  • People want to engage with nature, not just walk past it
  • Cost uncertainty stops trips before they are ever planned
  • First-time visitors feel out of place without context
  • Rangers answer the same questions at every stop
Key Findings
  • Active interaction makes a visit feel worth it
  • People assume parks are expensive even when they aren't
  • Discovery needs a hook, something to look for or find
  • Repeat visits depend on there being a reason to come back
Who We're Building For
Primary Audience
People who are curious about nature but don't know how to engage with it yet. They want to go outside, they just need a reason and a place to start.
Secondary Audience
People who already visit parks but want a richer experience. Species ID, geocaching, and badges give regulars something new to do every visit.
Design Constraint
This has to work for someone on a tight budget as much as someone who isn't. Nature should not have an income requirement.

Product Decisions & Rationale

Every major product decision traces directly back to something observed in research.

Cost Estimator First
People don't plan trips they assume they can't afford. Clarity removes that assumption before it blocks action.
Gamification Over Social Feed
Exploration should feel rewarding without needing other people. Progress has to work solo.
Offline-First
If the product fails in the park, it fails entirely. Parks have poor or no cell service.
Guest Mode
Value comes before commitment. People should understand the product before being asked for anything.
Built Around Repeat Visits
Parks depend on visitors to stay funded and protected. The product only works if people keep coming back. Badges, new geocaches, and seasonal content are all designed to give people a reason to return.

Design Direction

The visual identity is pulled directly from the Texas landscape. The logo is a monarch butterfly perched on a bluebonnet spike, developed through an iterative AI-assisted design process across dozens of variations until it read clearly as an app icon at every size.

Two icon variants Sage green for light mode and marketing assets. Slate blue as the primary App Store listing. Both read as the same brand at a glance. Color palette draws from real landscape photography: earthy sage greens, warm soil tones, slate blue, and cream.

Risks & Mitigation
Adoption Risk

Getting someone who has never visited a park to download an app requires meeting them where they are.

Mitigation: Park partnerships that put Bluebonnet in front of visitors who are already there, letting the in-park experience drive downloads and word of mouth.

Data Accuracy

Species identification and safety information need to be reliable or the app loses trust fast.

Mitigation: Building ranger input and community reporting into the core data model rather than relying solely on AI identification.

Long-Term Vision

Building toward nonprofit status with funding from donations, corporate sponsorships, and park partnerships. The goal was never to build something people pay for out of pocket. It was to build something parks and conservation organizations would want to fund because it genuinely gets people outside.
